Conway's Game of Life

Introduction

The Game of Life is a cellular automaton devised by British Mathematican John H. Conway. There are no players, and each subsequent evolution of the cells in the "game" is determined by the original configuration of the game. You are merely an observer in how the Game of Life evolves.

Rules to the Game of Life

The Game exists in a universe of a two-dimensional orthogonal grid of "cells," each of which is in one of two possible states: alive or dead. Every cell interacts with eight of its neighbors (top left, top, top right, immediate left, immediate right, bottom left, bottom, bottom right) in the following ways:

  • 1. Any live cell with fewer than two live neighbours dies, as if caused by under-population.
  • 2. Any live cell with two or three live neighbours lives on to the next generation.
  • 3. Any live cell with more than three live neighbours dies, as if by over-population.
  • 4. Any dead cell with exactly three live neighbours becomes a live cell, as if by reproduction.

Why?

The impetus for the Game of Life was due to a problem presented by John von Neumann in the 1940s. von Neumann tried to find a hypothetical machine that could build copies of itself (von Neumann celluar automoton / theory of self-reproducing automata). The Game of Life was Conway's attempt at simplifying von Neumann's ideas.



"He choose the rules carefully after trying many other possibilities, some of which caused the cells to die too fast and others which caused too many cells to be born. Life balances these tendencies, making it hard to tell whether a pattern will die out completely, form a stable population, or grow forever." [source]



The Game of Life can get quite complex; logic gates of AND, OR, and NOT can be programmed inside the Game of Life. A computer can be built inside the Game of Life universe given the correct starting configuration. Gliders and ship shapes can be used as electric signals to transfer information. There are examples of computers that have been built inside Life, such as a computer that can output prime numbers. John von Neumann showed that "a cellular automaton that could contain general self-replicating machines was universal" (Moravec, Mind Children, p.150).



Game of Life is a Turing Complete language. In computability theory, a system of data-manipulation rules (such as a computer's instruction set, a programming language, or a cellular automaton) is said to be Turing complete or computationally universal if it can be used to simulate any single-taped Turing machine.
